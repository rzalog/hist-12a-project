The Plantation to Prison Theory

The Plantation to Prison Theory states that when you compare the current state of mass incarceration with that of the slavery and Jim Crow era, a direct line between them can be drawn. In other words, the Plantation to Prison Theory serves to state that, to a certain extent, slavery still exists in the modern era, it has just appeared in a different form.

How does one draw a line between the slavery and Jim Crow era to today? Wasn't slavery abolished at the end of the American Civil War with the 13th, 14th, and 15th Amendments? Well when the two periods are placed side by side, a couple very defining similarities can be seen between them. By analyzing these similarities and differences, one can see that the conditions a person of color enduring during the slavery or Jim Crow era are not too different from the conditions of the modern criminal justice system. However, the Plantation to Prison theory isn't without flaws. There are several counter arguments and criticisms of this theory that attempt to disprove the theory.

Slavery and the Jim Crow Era

Slaves were a cheap source of labor for slave owners, and were critical within the US Southern economy before the Civil War.

Slaves were not allowed to vote during slavery (except for the 3/5th Compromise) and were further deterred from voting through mechanisms such as poll taxes and literacy tests.

Black people didn't hold any power within the court and were charged severely for crimes committed against a white person.

Despite being "separate but equal", black people were portrayed as being less fortunate and less deserving than the white population.

Modern US Criminal Justice System

Incarcerated inmates are forced to produce goods for no compensation.

Incarcerated individuals are not allowed to vote (in some states).

Policing (and predictive policing) and heavily skewed to incarcerating people of color.

Incarceration or even deportation used as a form of punishment to crime.

Acts such as anti-homelessness, anti-vagrancy, and the War on Drugs have portrayed black populations as more likely to commit crimes and deal drugs.
